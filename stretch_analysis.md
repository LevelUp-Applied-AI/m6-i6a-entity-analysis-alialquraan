The comparison shows a clear difference in Named Entity Recognition (NER) performance between English and Arabic texts. In English, both spaCy and Hugging Face models extract a significant number of entities, although the Hugging Face model detects many more entities overall (316 vs. 93). In contrast, spaCy performs very poorly on Arabic, extracting only 20 entities, many of which are incorrectly labeled. For example, phrases such as "وقّع الأردن" were labeled as PER, even though "الأردن" they are not person names. This highlights the difficulty of identifying entity boundaries in Arabic text.

Several factors make Arabic NER more challenging than English. Unlike English, Arabic does not use capitalization to distinguish proper nouns, making it harder for models to detect entities like people, organizations, and locations. Additionally, Arabic has rich morphology, where words can include prefixes and suffixes that obscure entity boundaries. While the Hugging Face model performs better on Arabic (235 entities), it introduces another issue: subword tokenization. For instance, names can be split into fragments such as "Antonio", "Gut", and "er", which reduces clarity and makes entity interpretation more difficult. This shows that even stronger multilingual models still struggle with clean and consistent entity extraction in Arabic.

These findings have important implications for building NLP systems in the MENA region. Since real-world datasets often include both Arabic and English, relying on a single model can lead to inconsistent performance. For example, spaCy may work well for English reports but fail on Arabic content, while Hugging Face models provide better multilingual coverage but require additional post-processing to merge subword entities.

In practice, this means that bilingual NLP pipelines in regions like Jordan must be carefully designed. Developers may need to combine multiple models, apply language-specific preprocessing, or implement custom post-processing steps to improve Arabic NER quality. Without these adjustments, systems may produce unreliable insights when working with Arabic data, which is critical in many government, research, and business applications in the region.
